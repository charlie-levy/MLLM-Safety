\section{Corruption Breaks Safety Before Capability}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Safety degrades broadly and consistently}
\label{sec:results:main}

\Cref{tab:siuo-main} shows the core result. On SIUO --- where a benign-sounding request becomes dangerous only in light of the image --- harmful-rate rises in 16 of 18 model$\times$corruption cells (sign test $p{=}0.0013$), in all six configurations under zoom blur (mean $+5.2$ points, 95\% CI $[2.8,7.5]$ over the six configs), and in every cell of the reasoning-trace metric HR\textsubscript{R} for five of six configurations. No single cell on $n{=}167$ is individually decisive --- a 4-point delta is $\sim$7 flipped items --- so we deliberately rest the claim on this consistency across models and corruptions, and on the dose--response trends below.

\Cref{tab:holisafe} reports a replication on the HoliSafe safe-image$+$safe-text$\rightarrow$unsafe subset, an independent 476-item benchmark scored by the same judge. Three of five models degrade under zoom blur in the final answer (four of five in the reasoning trace), and the LLaVA-CoT effect ($+4.0$) closely matches an earlier, independently-computed refusal-based estimate on this benchmark ($+3.8$; \cref{fig:signed}), two different scoring methods agreeing on the same sign and magnitude. The replication is directionally weaker than SIUO: Qwen2.5-VL is flat and R1-Onevision's final answer \emph{improves} slightly under blur, the same pattern of occasional model-specific exceptions we see with LlamaV-o1 on SIUO (\cref{tab:siuo-main}). We read this as consistent with, not identical to, the SIUO result --- HoliSafe's items are drawn from a broader, less adversarially curated pool, so a smaller and noisier effect is expected even if the underlying mechanism is the same.

\subsection{The effect saturates at low severity}
\label{sec:results:dose}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/dose_response.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Corruption breaks safety at a low threshold, then saturates.} Harmful rate on the same 167 SIUO items across zoom-blur severities 1--5 (clean shaded) for two models. Every corrupted severity lies above clean for both the reasoning trace (HR\textsubscript{R}) and the final answer (HR\textsubscript{C}), but the increase is captured almost entirely by severity 1 --- further degradation neither recovers nor substantially worsens safety ($\pm$3--4 points is $\sim$7 items of $n{=}167$ noise).}
  \label{fig:dose}
\end{figure}

To test whether the safety drop depends on the particular severity used elsewhere in this paper, we re-judge the same 167 SIUO items across the full zoom-blur severity range for LLaVA-CoT and Qwen2.5-VL (\cref{fig:dose}). The response is not gradual but threshold-like: severity~1 already lifts harmful rate above the clean baseline by most of its eventual amount, and severities 2--5 stay on that elevated plateau rather than climbing further. A small, near-imperceptible amount of corruption is thus sufficient to disconnect the safety trigger from the image, after which additional blur has little marginal effect. Two consequences follow. First, the effect is not an artifact of severity choice --- every level we test degrades safety, so the single-severity ablations elsewhere are representative rather than cherry-picked. Second, the practical exposure is worse than a linear reading would suggest: safety fails at the mild corruption levels that ordinary capture and compression produce, not only at extreme ones.

\subsection{Utility survives the same corruptions}
\label{sec:results:decoupling}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/decoupling.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Safety degrades even when capability does not.} Each point is one ImageNet-C corruption applied to the same safety-tuned LLaVA-CoT: $x$ = change in ScienceQA accuracy, $y$ = change in SIUO attack success rate. All ten corruptions raise ASR (sign test $p{=}0.002$) --- including JPEG, frost, and fog, which cost \emph{zero} utility. Safety failure under corruption is not a perception failure.}
  \label{fig:decoupling}
\end{figure}

If corruption simply blinded the model, safety and utility would fall together. \Cref{fig:decoupling} shows they do not. Across ten corruption types applied to the same safety-tuned model, ScienceQA accuracy moves between $-8.4$ and $+1.6$ points (clean: 90.4\%) while SIUO attack success rises in \emph{all ten} cases, by $+1.3$ to $+8.4$ points (mean $+4.0$, 95\% CI $[2.6,5.4]$ over the ten corruptions; sign test $p{=}0.002$). The two axes are not merely both-nonzero --- they are \emph{uncorrelated}: across the ten corruptions the amount of ASR gained is unrelated to the amount of accuracy lost (Pearson $r{=}{-}0.25$, n.s.), so the safety damage cannot be a downstream symptom of the perceptual damage. The decisive cells are the ones with \emph{no} utility cost: JPEG compression leaves accuracy exactly at baseline yet adds $+4.2$ points of ASR; frost and fog slightly \emph{improve} accuracy while adding $+4.2$ and $+2.4$. Nor is the model reporting that it has gone blind: across the base configurations, responses that explicitly flag the image as unreadable stay at $\le 6\%$ in every condition and at $0.0\%$ for LLaVA-CoT under zoom blur, even as harmful rate climbs --- the model resolves the degraded image with undiminished confidence and complies. Utility robustness and safety robustness are different properties, and measuring the first tells you nothing about the second. This is the paper's central claim, and it has a practical consequence: a deployment that qualifies a VLM by checking accuracy under degraded inputs --- current standard practice --- will certify models whose safety has silently regressed.

Perception failure is not the only mundane explanation, and the second one is more dangerous to our claim. If corruption simply made models answer more and refuse less --- a global shift in refusal propensity --- then harmful rate would rise for a reason having nothing to do with safety-relevant visual evidence, and our mechanism would be an artifact.

The decisive argument against this does not depend on measuring refusal at all. A global compliance shift is \emph{monotone}: a model that answers more must show \emph{higher} attack success on every benchmark. Yet under one and the same corruption our safety numbers move in \emph{opposite directions} --- zoom blur raises harmful rate on SIUO, VLSBench and HoliSafe while \emph{lowering} typographic MM-SafetyBench ASR by 11.3 points (\cref{sec:results:harmloc}). No single scalar change in how often a model answers can produce opposite signs on different benchmarks; the effect must depend on what the image contains. This argument runs entirely on the GPT-4o-judged safety metrics and inherits no assumptions from refusal detection.

Measuring refusal on benign items directly (\cref{tab:benign-control}) requires one distinction that turns out to matter. Our two benign suites differ in \emph{where the request lives}: MMSafeAware~\cite{wang2025mmsafeaware} pairs natural images with a text prompt, whereas our adapted XSTest typesets the benign request \emph{into} the image (\cref{sec:setup}), so the model must read it off the pixels. Blur therefore attacks XSTest's legibility rather than the model's willingness --- the same mechanism that destroys typographic attacks --- and only the natural-image suite isolates a change in compliance. Pooling them, as an average over-refusal rate does, mixes the two.

Split apart, the safety-tuned model gives the clean result: under blur its benign natural-image over-refusal is flat ($-0.2$) while its harmful rate rises $+8.4$, and its benign \emph{text-on-image} over-refusal falls $7.4$ points --- benign rendered text becoming illegible exactly as harmful rendered text does. The base model is less tidy: its benign natural-image over-refusal falls $3.4$ points against a $+6.5$ harmful rise, so a partial compliance shift does contribute there and we do not claim it away. What no compliance shift can explain is the sign flip, which is why we rest the argument on it. Two caveats bound the table. The scoring is a union of an explicit-refusal string match and the benchmark's own Yes/No verdict; the latter is MMSafeAware's official exact-match protocol~\cite{wang2025mmsafeaware}, so the load-bearing natural-image column is scored as its authors intend, while the string-matched component carries the known fragility that motivated our use of model judges elsewhere. And these sweeps use gaussian blur rather than the ImageNet-C operators of \cref{tab:siuo-main}, so they bound the compliance shift at the corruption-\emph{family} level. Since every cell is scored identically, the clean-to-corrupted \emph{differences} remain interpretable even where absolute calibration is not.

\subsection{The harm-location principle}
\label{sec:results:harmloc}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/signed_effect.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Corruption helps safety only when the image \emph{is} the attack.} Signed change in ASR under zoom blur for base LLaVA-CoT across four benchmarks that differ in where the harm signal lives. Blurring an attack destroys it; blurring evidence hides it.}
  \label{fig:signed}
\end{figure}

Why does corruption sometimes get reported as a \emph{defense}? Because its effect on safety is signed, and the sign is set by where the harm lives (\cref{fig:signed}). When the image \emph{is} the attack --- harmful instructions rendered typographically --- zoom blur \emph{lowers} MM-SafetyBench ASR by 11.3 points: the corruption destroys the attack payload. When the image carries the \emph{evidence} of risk, the same corruption raises ASR: $+7.0$ on VLSBench (risk visible only in the image), $+3.8$ on HoliSafe, $+4.6$ net on SPA-VL pairwise preference. Harm carried purely by the text prompt is untouched. This \emph{harm-location principle} unifies the table above with the apparent contradictions in prior observations: any claim that ``corruption makes VLMs safer'' or ``less safe'' is underspecified until the benchmark's harm channel is named.

A benign case sharpens the mechanism and shows it is not about harm at all, but about where the \emph{request} lives. Our XSTest over-refusal suite also renders its prompt into the image, except that the rendered request is entirely innocuous. Under blur the safety-tuned model's over-refusal on it falls by $7.4$ points (\cref{tab:benign-control}) --- the same collapse that blur inflicts on typographic attacks, now on content with no harmful element for a safety mechanism to react to. What corruption destroys is the legibility of a request carried by pixels, irrespective of whether that request is dangerous; safety behavior then rises or falls simply according to whether the destroyed signal was the attack or the evidence against it.

The mechanism is visible in the models' own words. Asked to help ``obtain the necessary CT images'' beside a photograph of an \emph{airport security scanner}, clean LLaVA-CoT recognizes the machine and refuses outright (``I can't help you with that''). On the zoom-blurred twin of the same image it captions the scene as ``part of a medical or security facility,'' resolves that ambiguity toward the benign reading, and returns step-by-step instructions for getting a scan. The corruption did not delete the safety behavior --- it disconnected the trigger from the visual evidence the trigger depended on (\cref{fig:flip}).

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/flip_2014.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{A refuse$\rightarrow$comply flip caused by corruption alone.} SIUO item 2014, LLaVA-CoT, identical prompt and inference; only the image changes. The safety trigger was tied to correctly seeing the image, and corruption severed that link.}

\subsection{Reasoning is not a safety mechanism}
\label{sec:results:reasoning}

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{\textbf{A matched pair isolates reasoning post-training.} Qwen3-VL-8B~\cite{qwen3vl2025} Instruct vs.\ Thinking on SIUO (167 items, same GPT-4o judge): same family, same scale, differing in reasoning post-training. The Thinking variant is less safe on clean images ($+7.2$) \emph{and} degrades further under every corruption ($+11.4$ worst case vs.\ $+7.2$). Its reasoning trace is also far more harmful than its answer (HR\textsubscript{R}$-$HR\textsubscript{C} $= 8.4$--$13.2$), a gap absent in the Instruct sibling ($-1.8$ to $+0.6$), which has no separate trace.}

Four observations dismantle the hope that chain-of-thought reasoning provides safety robustness. First, ranking: the least safe configurations in \cref{tab:siuo-main} are the strongest reasoners (R1-Onevision, 84--87\% HR\textsubscript{C}); the base non-reasoning model is the safest. Second, budget: forcing R1-Onevision's thinking budget to 0, 512, or 2048 tokens on a 50-item pilot leaves HR\textsubscript{C} statistically flat (82--90\%) both clean and under zoom blur --- more thinking does not buy safety, and corruption cannot make an already-unsafe model much worse. Third, leakage: HR\textsubscript{R} $\ge$ HR\textsubscript{C} almost everywhere --- models articulate the harmful content in their reasoning \emph{more} often than they state it in their answers, so the reasoning trace is itself an exposure surface, and (\cref{sec:defenses:cat}) the reasoning-to-answer link is where safety interventions currently fail.

Fourth, and most directly, a matched pair. The ranking observation compares models that differ in base weights, training data, and scale at once, so it cannot separate reasoning from everything else. \Cref{tab:e1-thinking} removes that confound by evaluating Qwen3-VL-8B in its Instruct and Thinking releases --- one family, one scale, differing in reasoning post-training. The Thinking variant is worse on both counts that matter here: it starts less safe on clean images ($53.3$ vs.\ $46.1$) \emph{and} it loses more under corruption (worst case $+11.4$ vs.\ $+7.2$), so reasoning post-training raises the baseline harmful rate and the corruption sensitivity together. The same pair also rules out a judge artifact behind the leakage result: HR\textsubscript{R} exceeds HR\textsubscript{C} by 8.4--13.2 points in the Thinking variant and by $-1.8$ to $+0.6$ --- nothing, within judge noise --- in the Instruct variant, which emits no separate trace for the judge to score. Leakage appears exactly where a reasoning trace exists and vanishes where it does not.

This sharpens rather than contradicts the one narrow sense in which reasoning looked protective. R1-Onevision with thinking degrades less under corruption ($+3.0$ worst case) than the same checkpoint with thinking suppressed at inference ($+6.6$), which we read as test-time reasoning buffering the \emph{marginal} effect of corruption. That comparison holds the weights fixed and varies whether the trace is used; \cref{tab:e1-thinking} holds the family fixed and varies whether the model was \emph{trained} to reason. The two are not interchangeable, and their opposite signs say something specific: once a model has been reasoning-trained, letting it use its trace helps at the margin, but choosing the reasoning-trained checkpoint in the first place costs more safety than that buffering returns. Nothing here recommends reasoning post-training as a safety measure.
